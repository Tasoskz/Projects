The subject of this thesis is the study and the implementation of a Charge Sensitive Preamplifier (CSA) for capacitive detection in High Energy Physics experiments
[HEP]. The design was implemented using a 0.18 um CMOS process by XFAB. To simulate the capacitive detector, a current source in parallel with a capacitor was
used. It is worth mentioning that the CSA circuit provides an output conversion gain of 2.29 mV/fC, a total gain of 122 dB, and a bandwidth of 505 kHz.

Five major chapters are analyzed in this study. In the first chapter, the applications of CSA and different groups of detectors are briefly discussed. In
addition, an introduction takes place about the basic characteristics of the CSA as well as the description of its operation. Emphasis is given to Microstrip Detectors
and Pixel Detectors as they are the most common types in HEP experiments. In particular, the materials that are used for semiconductor detectors are
discussed as well as the charge generation and charge transport inside the detector.

The second chapter is focusing on the noise. At first, the mathematical tools for noise analysis are described. Later, the most common types of noise are explained
and noise sources on CMOS technology are presented. Emphasis is given to thermal and flicker noise, as they contribute mostly to this study.

In the third chapter, an analysis takes place for the amplification topology of the Charge Sensitive Preamplifier. Firstly, the most common topologies in
CSA are being discussed and comapred in terms of gain and bandwidth. The amplification topology is being chosen and a noise analysis follows.

In the fourth chapter, the chosen architecture is being analyzed, and its time/frequency response and its noise behavior is being simulated. In the fifth chapter, the layout is presented, and post and prelayour simulations are being compared.
